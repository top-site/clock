üåç What Is a Time Zone?

A time zone is a region on Earth that observes a uniform standard time for legal, commercial, and social purposes. It is based on the position of the sun relative to a specific location on the globe. As Earth rotates, the sun appears to rise and set at different times across different longitudes. Time zones help standardize local time so that people across the world can coordinate effectively.

The Earth is divided into 24 standard time zones, corresponding roughly to each hour of the day, based on the Earth's 360-degree rotation (360¬∞ √∑ 24 hours = 15¬∞ per hour).

üß≠ The Origin of Time Zones

Before time zones, each city used local solar time, which made railway scheduling and national timekeeping a nightmare. Noon in one town could be several minutes off from another town just a few kilometers away.

The modern system of time zones was proposed by Sir Sandford Fleming in the late 19th century. It was officially adopted at the International Meridian Conference in 1884, where:

  • The Prime Meridian (0¬∞ longitude) was established in Greenwich, England
  • The concept of Greenwich Mean Time (GMT) was adopted as a time standard

This led to the creation of 24 time zones, each ideally 15¬∞ apart longitudinally from GMT (now replaced by UTC).

üï∞ UTC vs. GMT

Although GMT (Greenwich Mean Time) is still used informally, the official standard is now UTC (Coordinated Universal Time). Unlike GMT, which is based on Earth's rotation, UTC is based on atomic time and is much more precise.

Every time zone is defined as an offset from UTC, such as:

  • UTC‚àí5 (Eastern Standard Time)
  • UTC+1 (Central European Time)
  • UTC+5:30 (India Standard Time)

üåê Time Zone Offsets

Not all time zones are whole-hour offsets from UTC. Some use 30-minute or 45-minute offsets:

  • India Standard Time (IST): UTC+5:30
  • Nepal Standard Time (NPT): UTC+5:45
  • Australian Central Time (ACST): UTC+9:30

There are also regions that change their time zones for political, economic, or cultural reasons. For example, North Korea shifted to a unique UTC+8:30 in 2015, then reverted to UTC+9 in 2018.

üîÑ Daylight Saving Time (DST)

Some countries observe Daylight Saving Time, where clocks are set one hour ahead during summer months to extend evening daylight. For example:

  • The U.S. shifts from Eastern Standard Time (UTC‚àí5) to Eastern Daylight Time (UTC‚àí4)
  • The UK goes from GMT (UTC+0) to British Summer Time (UTC+1)

DST is not used globally. Countries near the equator often avoid it entirely, while others have abolished it in recent years.
